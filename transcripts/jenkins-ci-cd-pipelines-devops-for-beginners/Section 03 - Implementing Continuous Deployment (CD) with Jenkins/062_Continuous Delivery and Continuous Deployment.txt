# Continuous Delivery and Continuous Deployment

Let's go back for a second to the manual approval step to better understand why this is useful. Let's say we are managing a pipeline for deploying updates to a company website. This website is critical to the company's business, as it generates substantial revenue and is the primary point of customer interaction. But when would such a manual step benefit us? Typically, the following reasons are encountered. Most often, quality assurance is typically one of the main concerns. Even with automated tests, there can be non-functional issues or business implications that need human review. It can be, for example, visual elements or content that needs to be checked. A manual approval step allows for a final check to ensure everything meets the desired standards before going live. But risk management can also be part of deploying updates to a production environment can sometimes be risky. If something goes wrong, it could affect the user experience or functionality, leading to potential revenue loss and, generally speaking, damage to the company's reputation. The approval step acts kind of like a checkpoint to ensure most risks are considered and mitigated before deployment. Quite often, organizations really care about regulatory and compliance checks for certain industries. Changes may need to be logged and approved for compliance with legal or regulatory standards. The manual step ensures that all required checks are completed and documented. I can tell you from my own experience that larger organizations tend to generate a lot of paperwork before something is deployed, especially if they are not up to date in terms of doing modern DevOps. Also, such organizations tend to have a stakeholder sign off. Meaning changes that have significant business impact May 1st need approval before being released. This step allows relevant stakeholders essentially to sign off on the changes. Of course, there can be many, many other reasons, but let's zoom out for a second and take a closer look at the pipeline. What we now have is a fully automated pipeline. This means that the entire process of building, testing, and deploying the application is automated. This reduces the risk of human error and makes the process overall super efficient. We call this DevOps practice Continuous delivery. So even if we have a manual approval step, all pipeline creates production ready builds that are tested and that can be deployed with a click of a button without any additional manual work. When using continuous delivery, there should always be a build that is ready to be deployed to production. But there's another important practice you need to know about, and that is continuous deployment. Continuous deployment builds upon continuous delivery, but no longer has the manual approval step. So every change goes through the pipeline. And if all checks passed, that change is deployed to production. It is live for our customers to see. Essentially all large organizations such as Amazon, Google, Microsoft deploy changes to production multiple times per day, sometimes even multiple times per hour, without you even noticing. So to the continuous deployment we need to remove the manual approval step. Now in our example this is quite trivial, but in practice things are not always so simple. Typically, organizations that are just adopting DevOps practices tend to be a bit more reluctant to letting go of the control. Apart from this, our tests need to be solid, and we also might need to add additional safeguards, such as reverting to a previous version of our software if something goes wrong with the deployment.
