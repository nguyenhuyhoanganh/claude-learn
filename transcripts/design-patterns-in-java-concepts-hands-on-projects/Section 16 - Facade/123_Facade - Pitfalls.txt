# Facade - Pitfalls

Now let's discuss pitfalls of facade design pattern. Now, first of all, not the pitfall of the design pattern itself, but needing a facade pattern for a system that you are designing right now and implementing is typically a signal that you need to take another look at how you are designing your subsystem. If you are designing it and it is becoming quite complex to use for the client code, then instead of writing a facade, you should take a look at how you are designing your classes and interfaces and try to make them easier to use. Again, this is not the pitfall of the facade design pattern, but it indicates that in your system you should not typically need a facade. Now this design pattern is also often overused or misused pattern and it can hide improperly designed API. One of the common misuse that I have seen is that people use facade as containers of related methods, so they will have methods that are related to, let's say, printing and they will throw them in our facade. And these methods will simply call whatever classes they need from our printing subsystem. That is not what facade is meant for. Facade is meant to simplify the usage for the client code. Okay. So always be look out on such cases when you are doing code reviews. All right. So as you can see, these are not the pitfalls of facade design pattern because it is quite a simple design pattern and if you use it correctly, there is nothing wrong about this design pattern. Okay, so these are pitfalls for facade design pattern.
