# AWS IAM Identity Center

So now let's talk about the AWS IAM Identity Center. And this service is the successor to something that you used to know probably, which is called the AWS Single Sign-On Service. So the idea is that it's the same service, just been renamed and it gives you one login, therefore it's called single sign-on, for all your AWS accounts in the AWS Organizations, but also your business cloud applications. So you can connect to Salesforce to Box to Microsoft 365 and so on, or any application you want, as long as you have a SAML2.0 integration. And finally, it gives you also a single login into your EC2 Windows Instances. So one login, access to everything, which is really, really good. And the exam most likely will ask you about one login into multiple AWS accounts. Now, the identity provider, so where your users are stored for this login, can be twofold. It could be a built-in identity store in the IAM Identity Center, or you can connect to a third-party identity provider. It could be Active Directory, AD, or OneLogin or Okta and so on. So just to make it very visible and visual for you, we have the login flow, such as this. You go onto the login page, you enter your username and your password, and then you go directly into the AWS IAM Identity Center. And this is actually a screenshot from my own Identity Center page. And as you can see from there, I have enabled it for my AWS accounts. And so I can click on the account I want and connect directly, for example, in the management console. And then if I do this, then I'm taken directly from this into the home page of your console. But I'm logged in in that specific console. So the idea is that I don't need to know how to log in into that specific console. I can just log in into my IAM Identity Center portal. And from there I have SSO. So no need to enter your password once, it's single sign on, have SSO access to accounts, business applications, and so on. So if in the real world you have multiple AWS accounts, I would recommend 100% to use this service. Okay, so now let's talk about how this works. So the browser interface is going to connect to the login page of your AWS IAM Identity Center. And then you have to integrate it, as I said, with different user stores. So it could be an Active Directory, it could be on the cloud or on premises. And this is where you would use Active Directory to manage your users and your groups. Or you can use the IAM Identity Center. This is the built-in identity store. And this is where, for example, you would define your users and your groups as you're used to in IAM. Then you integrate Identity Center with SSO for your organization or your Windows EC2 instances, your business cloud applications or your custom SAML2.0-enabled applications. Again, the cool thing is that you have one login for all of this, and that simplifies a lot your flow. Now, of course, when you log in, you don't have access to everything. You define something called permission sets in the Identity Center to define which users have access to what. So how does everything relate in IAM Identity Center for permissions and users and groups? So we have an organization and we set up IAM Identity Center in the management account. We have two OUs, a development and a production OU, each with their own accounts. Now we have two developers in our company. We have Bob and Alice. So first of all, we're going to do, just like we do in IAM, we're going to create a group of developers for Bob and Alice. Now we want to make sure that Bob and Alice both have access to full access of the OU for development. So we're going to create what's called the permission sets, and we're going to allow admin access on it. And then you have to assign this permission set directly into specific accounts, for example, your development accounts, and then you assign this permission set either to your users or to a group of users, for example, your developers. So this would allow Bob and Alice to assume a role with full access on the development account B right now. You can also have a permission set, for example, for read-only access and assign this permission set directly into your production account, so account A and account B. And then you assign this permission set again to Bob and Alice, which would allow them to have a read-only access to these accounts. So now let's talk about the fine-grained permissions and assignments. And the first one is multi-account permissions. So, of course, using the service, you can manage access across multiple accounts in your organization. And using permission sets, you define one or more IAM policies that you assign to users and to groups to define what they can access in AWS. So here is the IAM Identity Center, and it's integrated with your organization and you define a permission set for database admins. So it's a collection of IAM policies. And you say, for example, that you have access to RDS and Aurora in your dev account, as well as RDS and Aurora in the prod account. And this will automatically create a corresponding IAM role for your users. And therefore, when you have your database admins, it can be a group, for example, and your users are in this group, you will assign it the permission sets of database admins. And whenever users log in through the IAM Identity Center and access the console of your dev accounts or your prod accounts, they will be automatically assuming an IAM role in that specific account that will give them the permissions they're supposed to get, which is pretty good. So it works the same way. So you have application assignments and you can define which users or groups can access which applications. And it gives them the required URLs, the certificates and metadata. So all of this is supported out of the box. And something that enables is called attribute-based access control. So the idea is that you're going to have fine-grained permissions based on the users' attributes stored in the IAM Identity Center store. So that means that you can have tags and so on. And using this, you can, for example, assign a user to a cost center or give them a title, so like junior, senior or a locale, whatever, so they only have access to a specific region. And the use case of this is that you actually define the IAM permission sets once and they leverage these attributes, and you only modify the access to AWS of your users or your groups by just changing these underlying attributes. So this is a use case that is advanced, but that is enabled by using the AWS IAM Identity Center. So that's it for this lecture. I hope you liked it, and I will see you in the next lecture.
