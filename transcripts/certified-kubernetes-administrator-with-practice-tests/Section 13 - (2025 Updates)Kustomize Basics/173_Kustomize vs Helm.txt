Now just a couple of other things to note about Helm. So Helm isn't just about customizing your configurations on a per environment basis, Helm is actually a complete package manager for your application. So think of like either YUM or APT for a Linux system, it kind of operates the same way for your Kubernetes application. And Helm is a little bit more complex, but it also comes with a few extra features that Kustomize doesn't have, so things like conditionals, loops, functions, and hooks, so you do get a little bit more functionality with Helm. However, with that extra functionality does come some extra complexity. So, you know, when you're dealing with these Helm templates, technically they're not valid YAML because they are using that Go templating syntax, and so if you take a look at some of the Helm charts out there, you'll see that they are actually really, really difficult to read, just due to that temping syntax where everything is a variable and it just becomes hard to figure out like, what exactly is our chart doing? What values can I replace? Overall, it just becomes a little bit of a challenge to read those charts, and that's one of the perks about Kustomize, is that Kustomize is very simple. It's very easy to read, it's just regular YAML. The regular base configurations is just regular Kubernetes configs, and then the overlays, where we go to modify it on a per environment basis, that's all valid YAML as well. So I think one of the big trade offs is, you know, Kustomize is easier and simpler, whereas Helm is a little bit more complex, but it has more features as well, so there are trade offs and you definitely wanna think about what each one brings to the table when you decide on what is the tool that you're going to use for your specific project.
