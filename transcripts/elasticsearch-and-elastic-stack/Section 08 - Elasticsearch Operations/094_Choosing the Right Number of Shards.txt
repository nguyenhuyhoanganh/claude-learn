# Choosing the Right Number of Shards

Let's dive into elastic search operations and how you deal with elastic search in the real world, and not just running on your own little virtual machine. One of the first decisions you'll be faced with is choosing the right number of primary shards in your index, and just to review a couple of slides from way back in the beginning of this course, as you'll recall, documents get hashed to a particular shard and you can have one or more than one shard and each physical machine in your cluster, usually that's dictated by how much total RAM is available on each node in your cluster and how many shards you can fit onto it. But, keep in mind, the shard is a self-contained lucene index of its own, so there is a fair amount of overhead associated with each shard, you don't just want to have a billion shards out there in order to future proof the scalability of your index. Now, remember too, that there are primary shards and replica shards, so going back to this example you looked at way back in and beginning of the course. In this example, we only have two primary shards primary shards zero running on node three and primary shard one running on node one. But we have two replicas of each primary shard running in this cluster as well, and that gives us a lot of fault tolerance in case one of these nodes goes down, but it also gives this more read capacity, because read requests can all be routed to either primary or replica shards. So really, the scarce resource here is primary shards, and that's in the context of writing data. So if you're in the business of just, you know, serving searches on wikipedia or something where you have way more reads than writes happening, you're probably gonna be good for a very long time because you don't probably need that many primary shards, your growth is primarily going to be on read requests and you can just keep on throwing new replicas at your index, as needed, as time goes on. However, if you're doing something like ingesting huge amounts of log it all the time, choosing the right number of primary shards gets a lot more tricky, because as your web site grows, you're going to need more and more primary shards to handle all the data being written into your index, okay? So remember; write requests get routed first to a primary shard and then replicated, so you need to have enough primary shards to handle all of your write requests at any given time, read requests can go anywhere and fortunately can add more replicas over time without re indexing. So how do you decide how many shards you need when you're actually setting up a new index? Well, there's no simple answer I'm afraid. Like we said, you can't just add more primary shards later without reindexing your index. I mean, that is an option though, like you know, it's not impossible to set up a new copy of your index and reindex it with more primary shards and then migrate your application over to that new index with more primary shards, you just don't want to do that very often and you have to remember too that primary shards are not free, you can't just make a thousand of them and stick them on one node because there's a fair amount of overhead associated with Lucene itself. So in general, you want to over allocate but not too much. The general advice here is to scale out in phases. So think about what your current requirements are for write traffic and read traffic and run some experiments to figure out how many primary shards and how many replica shards you need to meet your immediate needs, as well as the projected growth that you might have, for say, the next year or so, right? So, think about just planning for the next year and consider that beyond the next year, the next phase of your growth, if you get there, then that's going to be a good problem to have, right? And at that point, you can take the time to actually reindex your entire elastic search index and add more primary shards, it's painful but it can be done, and if you're only doing it once a year, well, that's what you're getting paid for, that's why you make the big bucks. So think about your immediate needs for scaling purposes, figure out how many shards you need to meet those needs, plus your projected growth for the next year, and if you do hit those targets, that's great, you can reindex and add more capacity at that time. Well, that is kind of a high end wavy answer; you're probably expecting like a secret formula or something to give you the exact number of shards you should allocate for a given index, but it just doesn't work that way, it really depends on the nature of your data on your application, the balance between read and write requests, how complex your schema is, how things are indexed, how things are mapped, how things are analyzed... all these add different amounts of overhead to your index. So, there is no one secret formula that you can follow to figure out how many shards you need, all you can really do is run a real world experiment. So, start with a single server using the same hardware you're going to use in production, with only one shard and no replication, fill it up with some real documents and hit it with real queries off line, so you can - you know - find tools or scripts that can actually simulate production traffic and throw it at your at your index here in a test environment. So just push it until it breaks, and at that point, you know the reach capacity and the write capacity of a single shard in your system, and you can extrapolate from that how many shards you would need to handle real production traffic, both now and in the foreseeable future, and use that number to figure out how many shards you should set things up for, initially. Now remember you can always add more replica shards later without any pain, as long as you add them on new hardware of course, adding more shards to existing hardware when your existing hardware is already pushing its limits, won't do much good. But the good news is that, if you're just adding more read requests to your system, you can just throw more replica shards onto your index and not have to re index it - it can actually take those on at runtime without reindexing.
