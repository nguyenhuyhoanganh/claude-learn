# Using Elasticsearch as a Vector Database with Embeddings and dense_vector

Elasticsearch has quite a few tricks up its sleeve. One of them is acting as a vector database or a vector store, whatever you want to call it. So if you need to do semantic search for a retrieval, augmented generation for some AI application, whatever it might be, Elasticsearch can do that too. What do we mean by semantic search anyway? Well, usually that involves something we call embedding vector or just embeddings for short. And that is a fancy word for a giant vector associated with some chunk of data. Maybe it's a piece of text, a word or a phrase, right? So think of it an embedding as just a point in multidimensional space. And typically that could be thousands of dimensions. But to wrap your head around it let's think about it just in two dimensions. So embeddings are computed such that items are semantically similar to each other and they're close to each other in that space. So things that kind of mean the same thing are close to each other within this embedding space. So for example, the words potato and rhubarb have embedding vectors that place them in that little lower left corner of this sample space because they are near to each other in terms of distance, we can say they are semantically similar. Same thing with this cluster of science fiction related things. Starship enterprise, Spaceballs, The Orville. Those are all close to each other because they have a similar semantic meaning. They all kind of mean kind of the same thing semantically. How does that work? It's kind of magic, right? How does it know what these words mean and whether they mean similar things? Well, that's where the magic comes in from AI. So you need to use some sort of an external model like GPT or something to compute those embeddings. The way that AI works and chatbots work and ChatGPT and stuff at the very fundamental layer, they have these embeddings that kind of encode the meanings of all these words and phrases, and that's how it knows what words and phrases mean when it's trying to put together a coherent response. So we're taking advantage of that sort of lower level of a large language model to compute these embedding vectors for us. So what do we do with those embeddings once we have them? Well, you're going to store them in a vector database. So they got to go somewhere where you can search for them. A vector database just stores your data alongside those computed embedding vectors. So maybe I have a vector that points to potato. Maybe I have some other data associated with potatoes that I want to retrieve with it. So a vector database can store that associated information with whatever embedding vector you have. And again these will leverage the embeddings that you might already have for machine learning or AI purposes. So sometimes you can pull double duty there. Retrieval however, is not going to look like a traditional search. So to look something up in a vector database, if you want to find things that are semantically similar to a given word or phrase, first you need to compute an embedding vector for the thing you want to search for. And then the process of searching is just a matter of querying the vector database for the top items that are the closest to that embedding vector that you want. So what you get back are the top k most similar things to that embedding vector of what you're searching for. We call that k nearest neighbor where k is how many results you want. So as an example, let's say that I want to search for things that are similar to a potato. I could compute an embedding vector for potato, and that would land it where that little rectangle for potato is. And then if I said k is one, I just want the one thing that's closest to potato in my vector database. Well that will come back as rhubarb because in terms of distance that is the closest thing to potato here. So there you have it. That's vector search and embeddings in semantic search a very popular application of this is what we call retrieval augmented generation. So the idea here is that well ChatGPT and large language models in general have a lot of problems. Right. Like they tend to hallucinate. They make stuff up sometimes and they don't necessarily know about current information or information that might be proprietary to your organization or to your application. The trick here is that we do a vector search for whatever the user is searching for on semantically similar information of your own. So if you have your own database that you want to draw from, you can say, okay, vector database, tell me things that are semantically similar to what this user is asking for, and then we include that information in the prompt that goes to the large language model itself. So we just say, hey, ChatGPT or whatever you are. Here's the original question from the user. Use this context that I retrieved from my vector database that's semantically similar to the question, and incorporate that into your answer. So that's a way to incorporate your own data into a chatbot or something. And in theory it can also reduce hallucinations because it has more information to work with. It's a simple example.
