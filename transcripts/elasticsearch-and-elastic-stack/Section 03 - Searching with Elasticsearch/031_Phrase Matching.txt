# Phrase Matching

Sometimes you don't want to search for individual search terms like Star or Trek or Wars. You want to search for phrases that are search terms together in a certain order, like Star Trek or Star Wars. So let's talk about phrase search and how that works in elastic search and some creative ways of using it as well. In Elasticsearch, it's pretty easy to just search for multiple terms together in the same order. All you need to do is say match, underscore phrase in your query instead of just match. So in this example, here we do a phrase match against the phrase Star Wars in the title. It's just that simple. The way this works is that in your inverted index, it doesn't only store that a given search term occurs inside a document. It also stores the order in which those terms occur. So you can actually use that information about the ordering of the occurrence of each term to piece together where phrases exist. That's how it works under the hood. It's very easy to use, but there are some more complex ways of using phrase search that let you do some pretty cool things. So let's say you care about the order of search terms, but you don't care that they're exactly right next to each other. There is something called the slop value and the slop represents how far too are willing to let a term move to satisfy a phrase in either direction. So if I were to say, for example, that I want to search for a quick fox, but I wanted the phrase quick brown fox to actually still match. I could say a quick fox with a slop of one. And for example, by saying star beyond with a slop of one that would actually match Star Trek beyond. But it would also match Star Wars beyond if such a film existed. So if you want to have a little bit of, well, slop, for lack of a better word, where you can have different words within your phrase, the slop value can let you do that. It can also let you do things like allow a reversal of a phrase. So actually Star Beyond would actually match beyond star if the slop was set to one. Sometimes that can come in handy to. It can also come in handy if you're just trying to do basically a proximity query where maybe it's not really a phrase you're searching for, but you want to give higher relevance to documents that have these two terms close together so you can do something like this. Let's say that I just want to get back results in order of relevance where Star and beyond appear close together. If I said Star beyond with a slope of 100, for example, a really high number that would give me back any document that has the term star and beyond within 100 terms of each other. But it would assign a higher relevance score to documents that have them closer together. So that can also come in handy. There are examples where you might want to search for two terms and not necessarily require that they're right next to each other. But the closer they are, the better.
