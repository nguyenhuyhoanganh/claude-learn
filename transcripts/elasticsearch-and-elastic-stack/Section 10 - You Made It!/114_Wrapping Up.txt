# Wrapping Up

Alright, you did it. You made it all the way to the end of the course, so congratulations, you've learned a whole lot during our hours together. We've covered a bunch of stuff at a very high level and you've also learned how to install elastic search itself, we've gone over mapping and indexing your data and analyzing it in different ways and how that all works, we've gone through a bunch of different ways of searching your data. We've gone through a lot of different ways of importing data into your elastic search cluster, being it from flat files wherever they might be stored, or even importing streaming data from other systems like Kafka and spark. We talked about ways of aggregating that data. Remember, elastic search isn't just for search anymore, you can also use it to aggregate structured data be a text based or numerical or whatever, and we've talked about the issues involved with both. We used Kibana to visualize those aggregations using a nice web based UI and we've used log stash, beats and the entire elastic stack as an all in one solution for collecting and analyzing and visualizing all of your log data from a fleet of web servers or some other fleet service for that matter. We've talked about operational concerns with elastic search and how to go about deploying your elastic search cluster. We've talked about what kind of hardware to use and things like that, and finally, we discussed using different hosted solutions for elastic search; we talked about using Amazon's ES service and the higher level elastic cloud as well, and the pros and cons of using each, and some examples of using them hands on. Wow, that's a lot. So if you want to learn more, there is more to explore. You can hop onto the elastic.co Web site, those are the people that make elastic search, so go to elastic dot co slash learn, and you'll see what more learning resources they have that are available. Some of it is free. For example, their online documentation is actually really, really good, their documentation writer is kind of awesome I think - and you can even get a print version of their documentation and a book from O'Reilly called elastic search, the Definitive Guide. Honestly though, I'd recommend sticking with the online documentation because elastic search is changing very rapidly, and by the time you buy a print version of that documentation, odds are there's a whole new version now that has made that documentation obsolete. The online documentation really should be your first line of defense. They also offer live training and videos at their Web site as well, it's not all free, but if you have an employer who's footing the bill for this sort of thing, why not? You can learn more that way, but above all, keep experimenting. Keep doing some hands on projects on your own. Think of a little side project you might be able to do or maybe a little freelance gig that you might be able to do for somebody you know, where you might be able to apply elastic search to an existing problem. Maybe you're working in a company that's already using a more heavyweight solution like Hadoop or spark for some problem, where elastic search might be able to do it much more efficiently. Imagine the hero you would be if you could take some job that takes hours to run right now and get the same answer back in just milliseconds with a little elastic search deployment that you set up in your spare time. In sum; the more hands on experience you can get, the more projects you can do on your own. That's all stuff you can list as experience on your resume to and that can lead to more and more career opportunities later on. So really learning this stuff and experimenting in online courses is all well and good, but you've really got to get some hands on experience to take this to the next level and really get an impact on your real career. So I do encourage you to keep on experimenting and find ways of applying elastic search to real world problems that you come across in your real world.
