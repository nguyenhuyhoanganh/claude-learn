# Design an E-Commerce Marketplace Platform 1 - Requirements & Sequence Diagram

Welcome back to another practice lecture. In this lecture, we will design another complex but real life system. As always, we will follow a very methodical process that will take us from the initial vague and ambiguous task to a detailed software architecture that satisfies all the functional and non functional requirements. So let's start with the general description of our task. Our current task is to architect a highly scalable eCommerce marketplace platform where different merchants can upload and sell their products and users can browse, search and buy those products. And just like before, we're targeting millions of users located in different countries around the globe. Of course, at this stage this may seem a very big undertaking given that companies that run such systems employ thousands of engineers and there are so many different components to such a system. But we are software architects and there is nothing we can't solve if we follow our usual step by step process. So let's start with the first step, which is clarifying our functional requirements. This will narrow our scope to what we need to care about, leaving everything else out of the picture. We first need to notice that in this case we have two actors in our system, merchants and buyers. So we need to clarify the functionality for both of them separately. For example, let's start with some questions about the merchant experience. Are we talking about digital products like ebooks or music or physical products with a fixed inventory? What kind of information does a merchant need to provide us when they're onboarding a new product to our system? What kind of data should we provide the merchant with about their product sales, if any? Or what kind of operations do we need to allow a merchant to perform on their existing products? When it comes to the buyer's experience, we can ask the following questions. Can anyone browse and purchase products on our platform or do they need to be registered users? Do we need to design a product review and rating system? What kind of product search capabilities, if at all, do we need to provide? What parts of the checkout experience do we need to design? And do we offer only a browser based UI or a mobile experience as well? So let's assume that after clarifying those requirements, we have the following information. First of all, the products were listing and selling our physical products with a limited inventory. Each product has a title description list of categories and images, but it can also contain additional attributes such as available sizes, colors, special features, and so on. For the merchant experience, we have the following two requirements. First, we need to provide the merchants with a product management system using this web based system. They can sign up, create new products, update the properties of existing products, update the inventory of an existing product, and also view their current product data. The second functionality is analytics. Using their merchant dashboard, they can view their current number of visitors to their products page in real time, as well as seeing the historical and projected trends about their products performance. When it comes to users or buyers, we also have two main functional requirements. The first one is a storefront in this storefront. A user of our web UI or mobile app can browse through existing products and also search for products based on their title categories and description. No user registration is necessary and product reviews are out of the scope for this design. The second functionality is user checkout. After a user adds items of existing products to their shopping cart, a user should be able to navigate to the checkout page. On that page, they will see the complete breakdown of how much they need to pay, including taxes. Once the user fills in the shipping and payment information, they should be able to complete the purchase with a click of a button. When a user completes the purchase, they should receive email updates and mobile push notifications about their order throughout the entire process. The shopping cart is 100% on the client side and is stored on the user's app or browser. And also we can offload the complexity of product delivery and payment to third party APIs. Now we have a much better understanding of what is in scope and more importantly, what is out of scope. But since we have two actors, multiple external service providers and so many different features, it may still feel a bit overwhelming. But that's okay. For such occasions, we have a great visual tool that we can use to organize our requirements, and that is a sequence diagram. So let's start with putting all the actors in our system, the merchant, the user, and the third party service providers, which are the payment and the delivery system. So first, let's start with the first functional requirement for the merchant. After the merchant signs up with our platform, they can log in and create a new product with an initial inventory. After the initial product upload, they can update their inventory at any time or update their product details such as description, title, pricing, or any other data at any time. For the second requirement, all we have to do is add the ability for a merchant to open their dashboard and send a request for the analytics on their product. Now let's jump to the first functional requirement for the store user, which is the storefront. When a user comes in, they send a request for the available products in our store. Of course, when implementing this API call we will use API pagination since loading all the products in our store on the user's device would be unreasonable. After that, the user can also make a product search to narrow down the list of products they're interested in. Obviously, the list of products returned by the query may also be very big, so we'll use API pagination here as well. And when a user clicks on a particular product, they should be presented with the full product page where they can add that product to their shopping cart. Now let's move to the user's second requirement, which is the user checkout. Once a user is done adding products to their shopping cart, they send us a request containing a list of products and their quantities. A response from our system should contain a full breakdown of the final cost, including taxes. After that, the user fills in their shipping and billing information on their browser or app. And when they finally click on the purchase button, a request goes to our system with all the filled in information internally. We record the purchase, update the inventory, and send the purchase confirmation response to the user. Then we build a user by calling the Payment System API and schedule the shipping by calling the Delivery Systems API. Also, at every transaction stage we will send an email and push notification to the user's device. Finally, once the delivery system receives and makes any progress in the product shipping, they will send us a message which will also result in an email notification to the user. And that's it. Now let's go ahead and proceed to the second part of gathering our systems requirements, which is clarifying the quality attributes for each use case for the merchant experience. Scalability is not a big issue. We'll likely have only a few hundred of merchants at most. And also those product or inventory updates will happen very rarely. Also, the overall total number of products will have in our system will probably be in the thousands or so. In terms of performance, we should provide our merchants with a decent user experience, but they can probably tolerate more latency than our end users. So our response time of under one second at the 50th percentile should be good enough. Regarding the storage we use for the products and inventory. We definitely prefer consistency and partition tolerance over availability and partition tolerance, since merchants always need to have the most correct data about their products. Finally, we do want to provide merchants with high availability, but just like in the case of performance, they can probably tolerate more downtime than end users. So 99.5 of uptime or close to it should be good enough. On the user side of things, we have a completely different picture. Scalability is of utmost importance. We plan to offer our products to tens of millions of customers in different countries, and when there are flash sales or holidays, we should expect very high traffic to our storefront and checkout. Also, performance has to be very high for the storefront. We need to provide the users with a list of products and all the product details at less than 200 milliseconds at the 50th percentile and under 500 milliseconds at the 99th percentile. The performance of the checkout should also be very high, but probably not as high as for browsing. So maybe keeping it under one second and the 99th percentile should be a reasonable. When it comes to data consistency. However, the store front and the checkout have completely opposite requirements for the storefront. We prefer availability over consistency to show the users some data, even if it's not the most up to date. But when it comes to product inventory, we would rather give a user an error in case of a network partition than build them for an item we no longer have in stock. And finally, we want high availability for both scenarios on such a scale. Every minute our store is done. We may lose millions of dollars, but since we don't have any contract with our users, we don't have to provide any external sales. But internally, we should aim for four nine of availability at the very least. So now that we captured all of our requirements and in particular the functional requirements, both as a general list of features and the sequence diagram, we can proceed to the next step, which is defining our API. However, thanks to the clarity we got from the sequence diagram, this step will be pretty technical, so we will skip it in the interest of time. Instead, we will proceed to the next step, which is translating our functional requirements into an architecture diagram. However, before we do that, let's quickly recap what we've done so far. We started with a very vague and ambiguous description of a system we need to design following our usual methodical approach. We started with clarifying questions to identify what requirements are in scope and more importantly, what requirements are not in scope either because we just don't care about them or because we can delegate them to third party services. Later, we created detailed sequence diagrams for each merchant and user journey. And finally, we captured a very detailed list of quality attributes that we need to strive for in our final design. I'll see you guys in the next lecture.
