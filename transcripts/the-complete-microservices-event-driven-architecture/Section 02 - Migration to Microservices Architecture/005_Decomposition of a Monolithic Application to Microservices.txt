# Decomposition of a Monolithic Application to Microservices

Welcome back. In this lecture, we will learn two methods to decompose a system into microservices that follow the three principles we learned in the previous lecture. We will first learn each technique in isolation and later compare them and discuss the significance of choosing one method over the other. The first method to decompose a system into microservices is by business capabilities. In this method, we observe and analyze our system. From a purely business perspective, a business capability is any core capability that provides value to the business or its customers. This can include things like revenue, marketing, customer experience and so on. Once we identify those business capabilities, we can dedicate a separate microservice and a team to fully own it. Now there are a few ways to identify those business capabilities. My favorite way is to run a thought experiment where you describe the system to a non-technical person and try to explain what the system does and what value each capability provides. For example, if we were to describe an online store, we would likely mention the following business capabilities the ability to browse through products using a standard web browser, the ability to search for products and view product details, read the reviews for each product left by customers who purchased it. Add the product to the shopping cart and place an order for that product and get it shipped to our address. We also need the ability to update and maintain the product inventory, both from the client's perspective when buying a product and from a merchant perspective that fulfills the order. So if we map those core business capabilities to microservices, we would end up with the following design. But how do we know that those services follow our three core principles? Well, first of all, just by looking at the names of the services, we can already see that this design follows the single responsibility principle, and that's by design, because each service is responsible for an entire business capability. We can also easily check if each service is cohesive by considering the changes that need to happen to our system. If we want to extend a particular functionality, for example, if we want to add new products or attributes to existing products, the only place we need to change besides the UI is the product service, but other services will remain untouched. Similarly, if we want to set new policies or features for how users live or filter reviews, the only service that needs to be updated is the review service. And if we want to change the third party credit card processing company we work with or add a new accepted payment type, the only service we need to update is the order service. We can also verify that those services are not too interdependent and are loosely coupled by reviewing a few user journeys or use cases. For example, when a user wants to load our online store, that request can be fulfilled by the web app service. When a user wants to search for a product or view a particular product, that request can be fulfilled by the product service. To look at the product's reviews, we only need to involve the review service and to place an order and ship the product. The only services involved are the order service, inventory service and the shipping service respectively, where each takes care of one clearly defined part of the transaction. Now let's discuss another way to decompose a monolithic application into microservices. This technique is called the composition by domain or sub domain. Unlike the previous technique that looks only at the system from the business side. In this technique, we take the developer's point of view of the system. In other words, the criteria for setting the boundaries of sub domains is based on the engineer's understanding of the system, which is why it is generally more intuitive. In this method we decompose the system into small sub domains where each sub domain is. Defined as a sphere of knowledge, influence or activity. Those subdomains can be of three types. A domain that represents a core business capability. A domain which is supporting a business capability and a generic sub domain. A core business capability is a key differentiator of our business from any other competitor in the field. It's a capability that cannot be bought off the shelf or outsourced. It's where we put our best effort and investment. Without it, our business doesn't provide any value. A supporting sub domain is still integral to our operation of our business and supports the delivery of our core domains. But it's not something that differentiates us from our competitors. A generic sub domain is a capability that is not specific to any business. Many other companies use it, and in many cases we could even get that component of the shelf. This categorization of sub domains helps us prioritize the investment into each sub domain. It can also help us figure out where we want to allocate our top and most experienced engineers and where we can save time or costs and get off the shelf existing solutions. Going back to our online store example, the core sub domain is clearly the product's catalog. Why? Well, because if we go to any other online store, we won't see the same products or get the same experience. That is why our online store is unique from any other in existence. To support the ability to sell those products in our store, we have the orders, inventory and the shipping sub domains. Without them we cannot sell products. But they may be similar in functionality to other online stores. Finally, we have the reviews, payments, search images, image compression, security and web UI. Sub domains which are generic sub domains. Those are still essential sub domains, but they are very generic because many other businesses, not even online stores need and use them. We also can easily get away with using off the shelf implementations of those components. Once we identify those sub domains, we have a few options. We can dedicate a separate microservice to each sub domain or if we see that certain sub domains are too tightly coupled with one another or aren't cohesive enough, we can group several sub domains into one microservice. For example, if we realize that the product image service and the image compression are too interdependent, we can group the two into the same microservice. Or if at the beginning we see that the payment service doesn't have enough logic to justify a separate service or it's too tightly coupled with the order service, we can also group them into one microservice. Of course, this isn't set in stone and if after a while we realized that now it makes more sense to separate them, we can always do so. And the small size of those microservices allows those migrations to happen very easily and quickly. As a side note, those two methods of setting the boundaries of microservices aren't the only methods. A few other methods include the composition by action on or entities. However, setting the boundaries by business capabilities or sub domains is the two most popular and easiest way to do so. So now that we learned the two main techniques to split an existing monolithic application into microservices, let's compare them side by side. Generally speaking, the first technique of decomposing by business capability usually produces more cohesive and loosely coupled services than the composition by sub domain. On the other hand, the microservices architecture we get from decomposing by business capability is usually more coarse grained, which means larger microservices. And when we decompose by sub domain, we tend to get more fine grained microservices architecture, which means smaller microservices. The architecture we get from the. Posing by business capabilities is usually much more stable. And that's because the core business is usually much more stable than engineering and technology. On the flip side, the composing by business capabilities requires much better understanding of the business. That means that this process will produce a less intuitive design for engineers than the composition by subdomain. Before we conclude, there are a few things I want to point out. First, it's important to understand that there is no one right way to set the boundaries of microservices, and what works well at the moment may not work as well in the future. So the architecture keeps evolving as new features come in and new business capabilities are added. There is also no perfect decomposition to microservices and some friction or coupling is inevitable. So the goal of setting the boundaries correctly is to minimize this friction and not completely eliminate it. Finally, the techniques we learned in this lecture are not bulletproof. They are just guidelines or tools to help us arrive at a good architecture. However, it's not a substitute for good engineering judgment and intuition. In this lecture, we'll learn two ways to decompose a monolithic application into microservices. The first way was by business capabilities, and the second was by sub domain. In the second technique we define three sub domain types. Core sub domains supporting sub domains and generic sub domains. Using either of those techniques helped us set the boundaries for microservices. Finally, we mentioned that none of those techniques is fully sufficient to guarantee flawless migration to microservices, and some engineering judgment is always necessary to make the final design.
